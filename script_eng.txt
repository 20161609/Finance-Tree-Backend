Hello everyone.

With this video, I’d like to introduce a web app that I built myself,
it's called Finance Tree.

This app is not just a simple personal budget tool.
I was thinking about small groups it's like churches, student clubs, and charity teams—
it s kind of organizations that need financial management with a big scale,
but they don't have any enough capacity for large and complicated financial systems.

In reality, even when the amount of money is not very large,
financial management in small organizations can become more complicated than expected.

[2]
For example,
some money may be donations,
some may be event expenses,
some may be meal costs,
and some transactions may belong to a specific department or a specific group.
so many cases are confusing..

But in many cases,
most of transactions are managed with a single Excel file,
with a group chat,
paper receipts,
or simply the personol memory.

Maybe There're going be several problems.

[3]
First,
the number of items,
it becomes confusing where each transaction should belong.

Second,
transactions keep piling up,
but later, it becomes difficult to understand
which department, group, or purpose for each expense was actually related to.

Third,
when it's finally time to report,
people often have to go back,
search through old records,
and organize everything again.

Fourth,
receipts and supporting documents also need to be collected separately,
which means that the person managing the finances
ends up doing a lot of repetitive manual work.

[4]
I wanted to solve that problem.

So with Finance Tree,
I designed the system to manage money
not just as income and expense,
but through a structure called branches.
Branches are really important features in this application.

In simple terms,
it allows users to build categories like a tree structure,
where smaller categories keep extending under larger ones like branches.

[5]
For example,
under a top-level branch like Home,
you could create major categories such as Worship, Mission, Education, or Events,
and then add more detailed subcategories under each one.

This makes the financial structure feel less like a long flat list,
and more like an organized tree.

Because of that,
users can immediately understand
which larger category a transaction belongs to,
and how deep it goes within the hierarchy.

Users can move through the branches they want,
view only the transactions related to that branch,
and also see their current location as a path.

So rather than being just an app for recording transactions,
I see Finance Tree as a tool for managing finances through structure.

When adding a transaction,
users can enter basic information such as date, amount, and description,
and also connect that transaction to a specific branch.

That means later,
the system does not just show that money was spent.
It also helps show why it was spent
and which part of the organization it belonged to.

I also wanted to support financial evidence and documentation,
so I added the ability to handle receipt images as well.

In particular,
I implemented an OCR feature for receipts,
so when a user uploads an image,
the app can automatically read key information
such as the date, amount, and description.

Of course,
OCR is not always perfect,
but I believe it is meaningful because it reduces repetitive manual input.

Another thing I considered very important in this app
was not only recording transactions,
but also organizing and reporting them.

In small organizations,
financial records often need to be explained to someone else.

For example,
how much money came in this month,
how much was spent,
how a specific category changed over time,
or how money flowed through a certain branch.

There is a limit to explaining these things only with words.

So Finance Tree allows users
to gather transactions by period,
analyze income and expenses by branch,
and review monthly financial flow.

And it does not stop there.

I also designed the system
so the data can be organized into report files.

In other words,
the goal is not just to view data inside the app,
but to make it possible to share, submit, or archive financial information in a practical format.

To summarize,
I believe this app is especially useful for the following users.

First,
people who manage finances for small churches or church departments.

Second,
people running clubs, charity groups, or small communities
who want to manage shared money more transparently.

Third,
people who feel that Excel alone is no longer enough,
and want to organize financial items in a more structured way.

Fourth,
people who want to manage transactions, receipts, and reports
within one connected workflow.

At the same time,
this app is not meant to replace a full-scale accounting system for large organizations.

Instead,
it is closer to a practical financial management tool
for small teams that need something realistic and structured.

While building this app,
my goal was not to create a complicated accounting platform.

My goal was to build a tool
that helps small organizations manage finances in a more structured way.

Recording money itself may not be the hardest part.

What is much harder
is leaving records in a way that people can still understand later,
and organizing them in a form that can be explained when needed.

That is exactly the problem Finance Tree tries to address.

It creates structure through branches,
connects transactions to that structure,
attaches supporting evidence,
and finally organizes everything into reports.

I built Finance Tree
because I wanted to make financial management for small organizations
more transparent,
more structured,
and less overwhelming.

Going forward,
I believe this project can be expanded even further
with features such as budget versus actual tracking,
role-based permissions,
approval workflows,
and collaboration at the organizational level.

In this video,
I introduced Finance Tree
by focusing on the problem it tries to solve
and the kinds of users it can be useful for.

Thank you for watching.
